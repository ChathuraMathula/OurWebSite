In this section you are going to learn the Basic rules of differentiation.

Here, \(f(x)\) is the function that is going to be differentiated and \(f^{\prime}(x)\) is denoted by the result after \(f(x)\) is differentiated with respect to \(x\).

In mathematics we call,

\(f^{\prime}(x)\) is the first derivative of the function \(f(x)\)

• There is a few of basic rules that you must keep in you mind when you differentiate a function.

The basic rules of Differentiation

  1. Constant Rule
  2. Constant Multiple Rule
  3. Power Rule
  4. Sum Rule
  5. Differance Rule

1.Constant Rule.

This rule says, the value of the derivative of any constant (denoted by \(k\)) is always zero \((0)\). If our function is, \(y = k\) (constant), $$f( x) \ =\ k\ ( constant)$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =0\ \ or$$ $$\frac{d( f( x))}{dx} =\ 0\ \ or$$ $$\frac{d( k)}{dx} \ =\ 0\ or$$ $$f'( x) \ =\ 0$$

2. Constant Multiple Rule

The derivative of a constant (denoted by \(k\)) multiplied by a function \(f(x)\) is constant multiplied by the derivative of the function. If our function(y) is constant(k) times another function(f(x)), $$y\ =\ k.f( x)$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( k.f( x))}{dx}$$ $$\frac{d( k.f( x))}{dx} \ =\ k.\frac{d( f( x))}{dx}$$ $$k.\frac{d( f( x))}{dx} \ =\ kf'( x)$$ Hence, $$\frac{d( kf( x))}{dx} \ =\ kf'( x)$$

3. Power Rule

Power Rule is about how to differentiate expressions of the form \(x^{n}\) Here, first you must take the power of the expression (denoted by \(n\)) and it should be multiplied by \(x^{n-1}\)
Let's take our function \(y\) $$y\ =\ x^{n}$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ nx^{n-1} \ ( n\ is\ a\ number)$$ Hence, the first derivative of any function of the form of \(x^{n}\) is \(nx^{n-1}\)

4. Sum Rule

The derivative of the sum of two or more functions is the sum of their derivatives.
Let's assume \(u\) and \(v\) are two functions of which the co-domain is \(x\). Alternatively we say \(u\) and \(v\) are two functions of \(x\) Then, by definition $$u\ =\ f_{1}( x) \ \ ,\ v\ =\ f_{2}( x)$$ differentiate each with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{du}{dx} \ =\ f'_{1}( x) \ \ ,\ \ \ \frac{dv}{dx} \ =\ f'_{2}( x)$$ If our function is the sum of two functions $$y\ =\ u\ +\ v$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{d( u+v)}{dx}$$ $$\frac{d( u+v)}{dx} \ =\ \frac{du}{dx} \ +\ \frac{dv}{dx}$$

5.Differance Rule

The derivative of the subtraction of two or more functions is the subtraction of their derivatives.
Let's assume \(u\) and \(v\) are two functions of which the co-domain is \(x\). Alternatively we say \(u\) and \(v\) are two functions of \(x\) Then, by definition $$u\ =\ f_{1}( x) \ \ ,\ v\ =\ f_{2}( x)$$ differentiate each with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{du}{dx} \ =\ f'_{1}( x) \ \ ,\ \ \ \frac{dv}{dx} \ =\ f'_{2}( x)$$ If our function is the sum of two functions $$y\ =\ u\ -\ v$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} =\frac{d( u-v)}{dx}$$ $$\frac{d( u-v)}{dx} \ =\ \frac{du}{dx} \ -\ \frac{dv}{dx}$$
Example 1
$$y\ =\ 7x^{2} \ +\ 5$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d\left( \ 7x^{2} \ +\ 5\right)}{dx}$$ By applying the Sum Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d\left( \ 7x^{2}\right)}{dx} \ +\ \frac{d( 5)}{dx}$$ By applying Constant Multiple, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 7\ \times \left[\frac{d\left( x^{2}\right)}{dx}\right] \ +\ \frac{d( 5)}{dx}$$ By applying Power Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 7\ \times \left[ 2x^{2-1}\right] \ +\ \frac{d( 5)}{dx}$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 7\ \times 2x\ +\ \frac{d( 5)}{dx}$$ By applying Constant Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 7\ \times 2x\ +\ 0$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 14x$$
Example 2
$$y\ =\ 10x^{3}$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d\left( 10x^{3}\right)}{dx}$$ By applying Constant Multiple, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 10\ \times \ \left[\frac{d\left( x^{3}\right)}{dx}\right]$$ By applying Power Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 10\ \times \ \left[ 3x^{( 3-1)}\right]$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 10\ \times \ \left[ 3x^{2}\right]$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 30x^{2}$$
Example 3
$$y\ =\ 5x\ -\ 1$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\), we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( 5x\ -\ 1)}{dx}$$ By applying Difference Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( 5x)}{dx} -\frac{d( 1)}{dx}$$ By applying Constant Multiple, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5\ \times \ \left[\frac{d( x)}{dx}\right] -\frac{d( 1)}{dx}$$ By applying Power Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5\ \times \ \left[ x^{1-1}\right] -\frac{d( 1)}{dx}$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5\ \times \ \left[ x^{0}\right] -\frac{d( 1)}{dx}$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5\ \times \ [ 1] -\frac{d( 1)}{dx}$$ you must keep in your mind that the 0th power of any value equals to \(1\) That is \(1^{0} =2^{0} =50^{0} =99999^{0} = 1\) Then, By applying Constant Rule, we have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5\ \times \ [ 1] -0$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =5$$
Summery
