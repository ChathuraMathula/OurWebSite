Trigonometry is concerned with the connection between the sides and angles in any right angled triangle
Trigonometric function can be defined in terms of ratios.

Let's consider a right angled triangle \(ABC\) as shown in the figure.

• The Hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle of a right angled triangle


$$sin\ \hat{A} \ =\ \frac{Opposite\ Side}{Hypotenuse} \ =\ \frac{BC}{AB}$$ $$cos\ \hat{A} \ =\ \frac{Adjacent\ Side}{Hypotenuse} \ =\ \frac{AC}{AB}$$ $$tan\ \hat{A} \ =\ \frac{Opposite\ Side}{Adjacent\ Side} \ =\ \frac{BC}{AC}$$
$$cosec\ \hat{A} \ \ =\ \frac{1}{sin\ \hat{A} \ }$$ $$sec\ \hat{A} \ \ =\ \frac{1}{cos\ \hat{A} \ }$$ $$cot\ \hat{A} \ \ =\ \frac{1}{tan\ \hat{A} \ }$$

1. Derivative of \(sin (x)\)sin x

$$\frac{d( sin\ x)}{dx} \ =\ cos\ x$$

2. Derivative of \(cos (x)\)cos x

$$\frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} \ =\ -sin\ x$$

3. Derivative of \(Tan (x)\)tan x

$$\frac{d( tan\ x)}{dx} \ =\ sec^{2} \ x$$

4. Derivative of \(cosec (x)\)cosec x

$$\frac{d( cosec\ x)}{dx} \ =\ -cosec\ x\ .\ cot\ x$$

5. Derivative of \(sec (x)\)sec x

$$\frac{d( sec\ x)}{dx} \ =\ sec\ x\ .\ tan\ x$$

6. Derivative of \(cot (x)\)cot x

$$\frac{d( cot\ x)}{dx} \ =\ -cosec^{2} \ x$$

Example 1
$$f( x) \ =\ sin\ x\ +\ 3$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\)x, we have $$\frac{d( f( x))}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( sin\ x\ +\ 3)}{dx}$$ By applying The Sum Rule, We have, $$\frac{d( f( x))}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( sin\ x)}{dx} +\ \frac{d( 3)}{dx}$$ $$f'( x) \ =\ \frac{d( sin\ x)}{dx} +\ \frac{d( 3)}{dx}$$ By applying The Constant Rule, We have, $$f'( x) \ =\ \frac{d( sin\ x)}{dx} +\ 0$$ $$f'( x) \ =\ \frac{d( sin\ x)}{dx}$$ By taking the derivative of sin x, We have, $$f'( x) \ =\ cos\ x$$


Example 2
$$y\ =\ cos\ x\ -\ 3x^{4} \ -\ 15$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\)x, we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d\left( cos\ x\ -\ 3x^{4} \ -\ 15\right)}{dx}$$ By applying The Difference Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ \frac{d\left( 3x^{4}\right)}{dx} -\ \frac{d( 15)}{dx}$$ By applying The Constant Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ \frac{d\left( 3x^{4}\right)}{dx} -\ 0$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ \frac{d\left( 3x^{4}\right)}{dx}$$ By applying The Constant Multiple Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ 3\times \left[\frac{d\left( x^{4}\right)}{dx}\right]$$ By applying The Power Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ 3\times \left[ 4x^{( 4-1)}\right]$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ 3\times 4x^{( 3)}$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( cos\ x)}{dx} -\ 12x^{3}$$ By taking the derivative of cos x, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ -sinx-\ 12x^{3}$$ Hence, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ -\left( sinx\ +\ 12x^{3}\right)$$


Example 3
$$y\ =\ 2\left(\frac{sin\ x}{cos\ x}\right) \ +\ 31$$ Because of $$\frac{sin\ x}{cos\ x} \ =\ tan\ x$$ $$y\ =\ 2( tan\ x) \ +\ 31$$ differentiate with respect to \(x\)x, we have $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( 2[ tan\ x] \ +\ 31)}{dx}$$ By applying The Sum Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( 2[ tan\ x])}{dx} +\ \frac{d( 31)}{dx}$$ By applying The Constant Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ \frac{d( 2[ tan\ x])}{dx} +\ 0$$ By applying The Constant Multiple Rule, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 2\ \times \left[\frac{d( tan\ x)}{dx}\right]$$ By taking the derivative of tan x, We have, $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 2\ \times \left[ sec^{2} \ x\right]$$ $$\frac{dy}{dx} \ =\ 2\ sec^{2} \ x$$
