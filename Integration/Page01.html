Introduction of Integration

❯❯

First we will look at a praph of a function of \(x\) drawn on a Cartesian Plane (\(xy\) - plane)
Figure 01 shows the graph of \(y = f(x)\) (A function).
Let's call it a curve.

Then, the Figure 02 shows you the area under the curve \(y = f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\). (That is from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\))
Let's call it \(S\). (To denote the Surface Area)

How we find the area under the curve ?
Think...!

So, that's why we need mathematical techniques.
The mathematical technique to find the area under any curve is called the Integration

Therefore by using Integration, we could find the area under any curve (any function) if you know the function.
*To see what is a function, please refer the Introduction to Differentiation of our Differentiation tutorials

Now we are going to learn the technique used to find the area.
We cannot find the whole area at once.
Hence, we devides the area under the curve and the \(x\) axis into very thin stripes as shown in Figure 03. And then we integrate each stripe's area and get the whole one. That means we take the sum of the every stripe's area.

Let's consider one stripe as in Figure 04.
We will let the breadth of this single stripe be \(\delta x\)
(\(\delta x\) means a very small value)

You will see that the height of the stripe at that point equals to \(y\).
(Because the value of the function is \(y\) at that point)
So,
$$Area\ of\ the\ single\ stripe\ =\ y\ \times \delta x$$ $$s =\ y\ \delta x$$

Therefore, the area under the curve is the sum of areas of all thin Stipes.
So,
Area under the curve\((S)\) = Sum of all areas of thin stripes
$$S\ =\ s_{1} \ +\ s_{2} \ +\ s_{3} +...+\ \ s_{n}$$ We can write it in mathematical notation as follows $$S\ =\sum _{i\ =\ 1}^{n} s_{i}$$
This is said as \(S\) equals the sum of all \(s_{1}\) to \(s_{n}\)
{\(\sum\) is the notation used to denote the sum of values and it is called Sigma}
Hence, $$S\ =\sum y\ \delta x$$
If we let this \(\delta x\) be very small, we can take it as\(dx\).
Then we use the notation \(\int\) which is a stylized version of the letter 'S' to denote the Sum instead of \(\sum\)

Now, the area under the curve \(y\) and \(x - axis\) is called the
Integral of the function with respect to \(x\).

One important thing is that,
You know that the differentiation (or finding the derivative of a function) gives the rate of change of something.

Here,
Integration gives the area or volume of something. Similarly Integration is the reverse process of Differentiation.
If we differentiation \(y = f(x)\) w.r.t \(x\) we get the result \(\frac{d( f( x))}{dx}\).
Then, if we integrate the result back w.r.t \(x\) we get \(f(x)\). $$\int \frac{d( f( x))}{dx} dx\ =\ f( x)$$

If we find the area under the curve within an exact region (which means we integrate a function within a limit(here, \(a\) to \(b\)) we call it a Definite Integral)

At the same times, if we find the integral of some finction without considering an exact region (which means we integrate a function without considering a limit) we call is an Indefinite Integral


You must add the word 'constant' each time you find an Indefinite Integral.
